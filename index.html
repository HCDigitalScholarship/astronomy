Expanding the Universe

The Telescope's Role in Revealing the Cosmos

  • Start Exploring

Introduction

The telescope’s centrality to astronomy is undisputable, but it was not instantly revolutionary. The first telescopes in the early 1600s were next to inconsequential when it came to expanding the visible universe. Astronomy not only requires the ability to see cosmic objects, but the ability to accurately position them in space and time. When you consider the size of our solar system, let alone the universe, it is understandable that rudimentary telescopes would have failed to reveal much. Expanding the Universe explores the telescope's shortcomings and its progress as an instrument, in cosmologies, in observatories, and in education.

Conclusion

Even without understanding astronomy or optics, it is possible to see why an instrument used to view objects light years away would need to be incredibly complex. The margin of error for detecting motion at that distance is so small that even the building’s movement matters. The telescope's role must be contextualized in order to appreciate its astronomical discoveries. Today, the remnants of our moon visits cannot be seen from Earth because even modern telescopes cannot resolve objects of that size from that relatively small distance. Assuming that early telescopes instantly revolutionized the way we viewed the universe and our place in it disregards scientific process.
